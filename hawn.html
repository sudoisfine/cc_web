HAWN

A truly odd, outré folk band, devout and radical in equal measure, HAWN crosses hot wires in the cool mortuary of Tradition. Soul, blues, badman balladry and campfire songs collide in a broad American vision that includes more than a hint of the Lynchian uncanny. Perhaps confused about proper instrumentation, HAWN ditches banjos, acoustic guitars and drums in favor of jacked electronics that gurgle, churn, and frequently erupt around Lee’s injured, near-cloying voice. Their precise, oddly propulsive songs advance with tragic certainty that’s quite formal--almost classical--but never arrive at a foregone conclusion, and are never without a deeply mischievous sense of humor. As Suicide was to rockabilly, HAWN is to folk. Scott Walker, and Big Star are also major influences.

Badmans

2014

HAWN’s second offering. Something of a synth-pop album, which also functions as a work of social theater, whose actors—menaced and menacing victims of all kinds of violence—plod through a beautiful and terrifying aural terrain, singing songs of personal damnation and small triumph. They’re aided musically by the strains of decrepit club beats, broken poker machines, flatlining EKGs, New-Age meditation soundtracks, and greasy gales of wind. Cold and aloof on its surface, grieving in its substratum, with a core of molten anger, Badmans offers a radically negative vision of the contemporary world.

Spree

2012

HAWN’s first full length, a real variety show. Seven dread reveries draped over blunt samples, unsympathetic guitar, and broken piano, with a singer so far gone he's wearing a smile nicked from Alex Chilton. Cast of characters includes bad angels, departed lovers, kind nurses, summer idlers, and helpful policemen. The sound of two individuals locked in a tower, making blues music to ferment their own bad dreams.
