AUDO

As AUDO, sound artists John Craun and Jeff Byrd weaponize synthesizers, sequencers, field recordings, and analog tape loops in their quest for a terrifying sublime. Creating all sounds “from scratch” during extensive improvisations, they hone these sessions into conceptually unified works—music at once formally rigorous and intensely evocative.

Homeskin

2016

A deadly, deadpan paean to the domestic, a piece in three movements which culminates in the apocalyptic mumblings of a numbed, fish-eyed narrator. Notable for its use of human language, Craun writes here in a surreal-poetical vein, the strange playfulness of which enters into sharp tension with the dread, volatility, and violence of the music.

The Remembery

2014

The Remembery is a symphonic synth and tape loop composition in three movements, beginning in the primordial dark and ending somewhere in the suburbs, a long night’s journey into deeper night or an early, nervous morning. Or, as one critic has said, “A piece of program music created by aliens to commemorate and mourn the rise and fall of the Earth species homo sapien.”

FUCKTOWN

FUCKTOWN is a collaboration between acclaimed poet Joseph Bradshaw and sound artist John Craun. Their eponymous first album finds the duo feeding Bradshaw’s screeds through a voice-to-text application and building visceral, demented, delightful soundscapes around them. Both collaborators reside in New Orleans—America’s most famous fucktown.

Fucktown

2016

Writhing in a furnace of unrelenting commodification, scatological hysteria, and acute sexual frustration, a haywire voice—let’s call it Joseph Bradshaw—boils off our most encrusted pretentions of authorship, identity, meaning, and taste. Meanwhile, Craun’s music—throbbing, cynical, chintzy, carnivalesque—mocks, reflects, and amplifies the poet’s insights and insults.

As a voyeur then accomplice in this flagrant act, the listener is finally engulfed, as the disembodied voice Joseph Bradshaw, unnervingly close now, levels a powerfully ethical judgement, a devilish j’accuse.

This secret (a guilty secret) between Bradshaw and the listener, hangs in the putrid air, as our man—ever at large—continues to Family Dollar for a fresh box of Cheez-its.

Bruce Haack and Throbbing Gristle are touchstones—so are Rimbaud and Artaud. An album that quite simply has no respect for anything.

HAWN

A truly odd, outré folk band, devout and radical in equal measure, HAWN crosses hot wires in the cool mortuary of Tradition. Soul, blues, badman balladry and campfire songs collide in a broad American vision that includes more than a hint of the Lynchian uncanny. Perhaps confused about proper instrumentation, HAWN ditches banjos, acoustic guitars and drums in favor of jacked electronics that gurgle, churn, and frequently erupt around Lee’s injured, near-cloying voice. Their precise, oddly propulsive songs advance with tragic certainty that’s quite formal--almost classical--but never arrive at a foregone conclusion, and are never without a deeply mischievous sense of humor. As Suicide was to rockabilly, HAWN is to folk. Scott Walker, and Big Star are also major influences.

Badmans

2014

HAWN’s second offering. Something of a synth-pop album, which also functions as a work of social theater, whose actors—menaced and menacing victims of all kinds of violence—plod through a beautiful and terrifying aural terrain, singing songs of personal damnation and small triumph. They’re aided musically by the strains of decrepit club beats, broken poker machines, flatlining EKGs, New-Age meditation soundtracks, and greasy gales of wind. Cold and aloof on its surface, grieving in its substratum, with a core of molten anger, Badmans offers a radically negative vision of the contemporary world.

Spree

2012

HAWN’s first full length, a real variety show. Seven dread reveries draped over blunt samples, unsympathetic guitar, and broken piano, with a singer so far gone he's wearing a smile nicked from Alex Chilton. Cast of characters includes bad angels, departed lovers, kind nurses, summer idlers, and helpful policemen. The sound of two individuals locked in a tower, making blues music to ferment their own bad dreams.
